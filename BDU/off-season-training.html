Winter Indoor Training: Keep Developing Year-Round

Winter doesn't mean your development has to stop. In fact, some of the most significant improvements happen during the off-season when players commit to consistent at-home training. I often emphasize keeping a ball near your feet during idle time—it's a habit that transforms good players into great ones.

Even today, when I'm on the phone, I still pass a ball against the couch just as I did when developing my own skills. The real magic happens when technical work becomes so natural that you can do it while multitasking. Watching TV with a ball at your feet, doing footwork drills during commercial breaks, or practicing wall touches while listening to music—these "invisible" training sessions add up to thousands of touches over a season.

Below, I've included training videos and equipment recommendations that can help you maximize your indoor development while protecting your furniture and walls from getting damaged. Think of these as holiday gift ideas that keep giving all year long.

These are starter sessions to build your foundation. If you feel like you've progressed beyond these drills, let me know and I'll add more advanced content. That said, here's an important truth: these skills are never truly "mastered"—they're disciplines we continuously refine. Even professional players return to these fundamentals regularly.

The commitment is simple: If you can dedicate just 15 minutes a day to these exercises, you're going to see real improvements. Consistency beats intensity every time. And if you only do this once in the next three months? That's still one more time than you did before—and that's progress. Every touch counts. Every session matters.

Wall Session for Pro Touch

Wall work is, in my opinion, one of the most underutilized training methods. You don't need a training partner—the wall never gets tired, never complains, and is always available. Any skill you learn can be incorporated into wall work, making it infinitely adaptable to your development needs.

This video starts with the basics and can be done in a surprisingly small space. Notice the pace in the video—that's the goal, but don't rush it. Start slow, master the technique, then gradually work up to game speed. The video also includes functional juggling exercises that translate directly to game situations. While traditional juggling has its place, these game-like variations are an excellent foundation for developing real touch and control under pressure.

5 Beginner Footwork Drills To Master

These fundamental footwork drills will improve your speed of play and ball control. The beauty of these exercises? You don't even need cones—any household objects work perfectly. Scatter toys around the room, use water bottles, or place shoes as markers to create your own dribbling obstacle course. Start slow, focus on technique, then gradually increase your pace.
